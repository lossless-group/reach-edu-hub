Opinionated Strategy · Grant Doctrine
Don't spray small applications. Concentrate where the money actually is.
Grant funding follows a power law: a small number of mega-foundations move most of the dollars, and a fast-growing population of ultra-wealthy individuals hold enormous discretionary capacity. The method is to reverse-engineer the few funders who can write the biggest, most-aligned checks — and design the ask to hit their sweet spot at the largest plausible size.
  • The 990 tells you what they funded; the call tells you why. Public footprint reveals the history; a conversation reveals the thesis behind the checks.
  • Start from what they can give, then shape the ask. Reverse the usual flow — design to the largest amount that still fits their pattern, not a default small request.
  • Effort follows dollars. A handful of precisely-aligned asks to the biggest, best-fit funders out-raises a hundred generic applications to the long tail.
01 A Power Law

Available funding is not evenly distributed. Of 120,000+ U.S. foundations, a few hundred of the largest move a hugely disproportionate share of grant dollars (Gates largest; Mellon ~$302M, RWJ ~$293M dwarf the median). A power law, not a bell curve.

02 Two Concentrated Pools
  • Mega-foundations — predictable, RFP-driven, deep capacity.
  • New-money UHNWIs — more discretionary, faster decisions, identity-driven; less predictable, relationship-driven.
  • ~$11.9T of the wealth transfer flows to charity (Cerulli).
03 The Method
  • Study the public footprint — 990s, Candid, press, principals' statements.
  • Get the intro call — the highest-leverage step; learn the thesis.
  • Pattern-recognize the recurring shape they keep paying for.
  • Align the ask to their sweet spot, sized up.
04 The Engine

This is what a funder corpus + agents are for. Reach already has a funder corpus under augment-it (dozens of funders, hundreds of documents). Reverse-engineering is the strategy; the corpus + agents are the engine that makes pattern-matching at scale tractable for a small team.

Chase the Few
Rank prospects by capacity × alignment and spend the team's scarce time at the top of that list. A few precise asks beat a hundred generic ones — and new money is more discretionary, faster, and looking for a cause to call its own. Reverse the flow: start from what a funder can give, then shape the program to match.
