# Pipeline Building & Automation

> Scroll-deck outline. Audience: Reach University senior executives — already in on the partnership, eager but behind the AI curve. We're wowing, not selling. This is the Lossless side of the work — what we'll build for Reach next, mapped to the *Build Smarter / Agentic Leapfrog* pillar with *Raise Better / Fundraise Acceleration* as the immediate use case.

## Narrative arc

**Empathy first → archetypes that re-key the work → the operating levers behind every recommendation → roster of who plays → help in two specific flavors → the stack that does it → the data fuel → widen the aperture → see the whole landscape on one multivariate map.**

The implicit promise: *we start by giving your existing team relief, we name the levers we'll actually pull, and we end by handing you a map of opportunity nobody at peer institutions has.*

## Slide 02 · Three Games, One Pipeline

**Header:** Three archetypes of philanthropy. Different rules. Different plays. Same underlying pipeline.

**Lede:** *Who shows up from Reach, what closes the deal, and what automation actually helps — all change with the archetype. Same pipeline; different surfaces light up.*

**The table** (three columns, same row structure across so the differences pop on a scan):

| | **Individual HNWI** | **SFO / UHNWI** | **Institutional** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **How they actually decide** | Solo. Sometimes family or friends in the room. Often a single introduction, a key meeting, and strong follow-up is the whole game. | A small team that *likes to pretend* they have process. Will run things like network-maven events. May put you in a decision pipeline. Rarely runs big grant processes. | Established teams. Real process. Deadlines, applications, multiple pitches and follow-ups — remote and at HQ. Rarely escaped. |
| **What can short-circuit it** | A baseball game. Golf. A well-placed dinner. | Bourbon with the principal — who verbally says yes, then doesn't want to "go over the heads" of the team and routes you back into the process you were trying to skip. | Almost nothing. The cycle is the cycle. |
| **Where the work actually happens** | Relationship logistics. Travel. Follow-up craft. | Materials for the team you got routed into. Personalization for the principal *and* the team. | Grant discovery → application drafting → tracking → close. |
| **Who from Reach plays** | CEO mostly. VP Development sometimes. | CEO + VP Development + occasionally one trusted staff. | Full dev shop: development officers, grant writers, program leads. |
| **What automation actually helps** | Trip planning. Call transcripts → follow-up drafts. Email CC capture. | All of the HNWI tools + personalized materials for the SFO team. Customized communication schedules. | The full grant pipeline: discovery, content-mapping, draft generation, deadline tracking. |

**Reader takeaway:** the same pipeline serves all three — but *which surfaces light up*, and *who from Reach is in the room*, depends on which game is being played.

## Slide 03 · Five Levers, Often Neglected

**Header:** Five levers. *Often neglected. Rarely well executed.*

**Eyebrow:** The unique-ish insights that drive and animate the recommendations in this deck.

**Lede:** *These are the operating beliefs behind every other slide. They shape what gets built, what gets prioritized, and where I push hardest when the room wants to default to something easier.*

### 01 · Planned Serendipity — *going for luck*

- Heatmap travel. Outreach for *anyone* from Reach visiting a location — events, meetings, even holidays.
- Create opportunities to stay in touch. Get to know each other. Stay top-of-mind without the meeting being "the ask."
- Small bumps in travel budgets and viable work-expense allowances go a *long* way.
- Involve more people from Reach than you'd think appropriate. Give everyone reasons to talk about you.
- Maximize inbound — SEO and GEO strategies — so aligned people find *us*. We don't even have to go find them.

> *Pairs with a follow-on slide: inbound vs outbound, framed via Predictable Revenue's **Seeds, Spears, and Nets**. See "Future slide candidates" below.*

### 02 · Authenticity & the Mechanics of Liking

- *Familiarity. Similarity. Frequency.* The three reliable ingredients of relationship.
- Work and personal relationships blur. Find authentic ways of befriending — don't pretend they're separate.
- The authenticity and care have to **overcome the tacit knowledge that "all this is planned."** Both are true; authenticity has to feel *more* true.
- Helps to have: a unique POV, a recognizable voice, articulated belief pillars, and clear impactful differentiators — not just from perceived competitors but from *alternative uses of a donor's time and energy*.

### 03 · Slow Down to Speed Up

- Build. Tinker. Experiment. *Then* codify and iterate on process mechanics and best-in-class digital systems tooling.
- The **Urgent vs Important** trap.
- The **Rainmaker vs Development** trap.
- The trap is always being caught up in short-term, in-the-moment priorities — so the long-term compounding never starts. Time invested in tooling pays huge dividends as time goes on.

### 04 · Pipelines Must Reflect Messy Reality

- The pipeline must reflect a messy reality — *not* be artificially simple.
- Simplicity can be an overlay *on top of* the complex reality, but the underlying data has to be honest.
- Overly-simplified pipeline dashboards become meaningless and unactionable — they get ignored within a quarter.

### 05 · The Coalition of the Willing

- Goes commando. Leads by example. Convinces by *doing* — by showing the work, the impact, the efficiency and productivity gains.
- Always welcome more of the Willing.
- **Don't let unwilling stakeholders freeze, pause, or delay initiatives.**
- If the Coalition moves fast, anything the skeptics doubted will either already be scrapped (a valid experiment, properly disposed of) or already iterated towards *wowing* the skeptics.

**Why this slide is here (after the archetype frame, before the roster):** the archetypes describe the *game*; these levers describe *how I play it*. Knowing the levers reframes the player roster on the next slide — especially "involve more people from Reach than you'd think appropriate."

## Slide 04 · Key Players We're Lifting

**Header:** Who gets relief, who gets leverage.

**Lede:** *Not every role plays in every archetype. We design the automations around the roles that actually carry the relationship.*

**The cast:**

- **CEO** — the closer. Plays in all three archetypes; carries HNWI and SFO almost alone.
- **Travellers** (any of them) — the people in the room when the room matters. Trip planning, transcripts, follow-up.
- **Most senior Reach Edu team member in any location** — the local face. Stewards relationships between visits.
- **Development officers** — the relationship-and-pipeline keepers. Heavy in SFO and Institutional.
- **Grant writers** — the engine of the Institutional game.
- **Involved donors** — the bench. Warm introductions, network leverage, board adjacency.

**Visual treatment idea:** small archetype chips on each role (`HNWI` / `SFO` / `Inst.`) showing where they play. The CEO has all three; the grant writer has one. This makes the dependency structure visible without text.

## Slide 05 · Donor List Personalization

**Header:** Tailor the conversation to the donor you already have.

**Archetype chip:** `HNWI · SFO` *(and the relationship-side of Institutional)*

**Lede:** *Enhance donor engagement by tailoring communications based on individual donor preferences, giving history, and interests.*

**The work:**

- **Angle hypotheses** — develop concrete theses for how to promote existing donors to larger donors. Why might *this* donor, given *what we know*, double their commitment?
- **Interest-based content recommendations** — match what we have to what they care about.
- **Personalized materials:**
  - Presentations
  - Landing pages, email templates
- **Customized communication schedules** — cadence tuned to the donor, not the dev calendar.

**Why this matters here:** the HNWI and SFO games are won on *personal salience*. Personalization isn't a nice-to-have — it's the whole instrument.

## Slide 06 · Augment the Grant Opportunity Pipeline

**Header:** Find more. Track more. Write better. Win more.

**Archetype chip:** `Institutional`

**Lede:** *Enhance grant opportunity identification and tracking by leveraging AI to analyze vast amounts of data from multiple sources — government databases, news articles, foundation 990s, social media — to surface relevant opportunities and provide actionable insights.*

**The work:**

- **AI-powered grant opportunity discovery** — a continuous scan across the sources that matter for higher-ed philanthropy.
- **Automated grant tracking and management** — deadlines, status, ownership, what's owed to whom.
- **AI-driven grant writing assistance:**
  - **Content Mapping** — align grant requirements with organizational capabilities. (*What does this funder want, and what do we actually have that fits?*)
  - **Draft Generation** — generate initial grant proposals from organizational data, ready for the grant writer to shape.

**Why this matters here:** the institutional game is volume × precision. The cycle doesn't shorten — but you enter more of them, on better-matched terms, with better drafts.

## Slide 07 · The Probable Stack

**Header:** The stack that does all of this.

**Lede:** *Not vapor. Named tools, named layers, real boundaries.*

**The layers** (translated to *what each layer does for Reach*, with the tool names as captions):

- **The institutional memory layer** — everything Reach has ever read, written, said about a donor, a foundation, a program, a trip. *Tools: KnowledgeBase AI / vector DB (e.g. ChromaDB) for RAG; a relational database app underneath.*
- **The thinking layer** — the models that read, reason, and write. *Tools: Claude Max, Perplexity Max — accessed through a unified API gateway so the org isn't locked into one provider.*
- **The doing layer** — the workflows that wire it all together. *Tools: n8n, Dynamiq, Flowise — workflow automation that runs without a developer in the loop for every step.*
- **The plumbing layer** — moving data between the systems Reach already uses and the new ones we're adding. *Tools: next-generation iPaaS such as Bruin.*
- **The seeing layer** — dashboards, maps, and reports. *Tools: visualization / analytics on top of the institutional memory layer.*

**Original tool list, preserved:**

- Database App
- API Gateway
  - Claude, Perplexity Max subscriptions
  - KnowledgeBase AI e.g. ChromaDB for RAG
- Workflow Automation via n8n, Dynamiq, Flowise
- iPaaS next generation, such as Bruin
- Visualization/Analytics

**Why this slide is here, not earlier:** credibility, not vendor pitch. We earn the right to name tools by first having named the work.

## Slide 10 · Geographic Cluster Analysis

**Header:** Where philanthropic dollars are pocketed.

**Lede:** *The geographic regions and clusters where philanthropic dollars are concentrated — and where giving in education is most concentrated — are where Reach Edu can have the greatest impact on the fundraise.*

**The work:**

- Map the geographic concentration of HNWI and SFO wealth in the US (and globally where it matters for Reach).
- Overlay where education-focused giving is concentrated — not the same map as raw wealth.
- Identify regions where Reach has under-deployed presence relative to opportunity density.
- Inform trip planning, regional event strategy, and where to seed local relationships.

**Why this matters:** travel and presence are expensive. A map turns "where should the CEO go next quarter" from a guess into a routing problem.

## Slide 11 · Theme Mapping — as analogy and literally

**Header:** Map the ideas the way you map the places.

**Lede (preserved from the original outline):**

> *Mapping themes is like mapping territories. Just as a map shows the boundaries and characteristics of different regions, theme mapping shows the boundaries and characteristics of different ideas and concepts. This allows for a more comprehensive understanding of the landscape and can help identify opportunities and challenges.*
>
> *Yet, as we will have their location, they can also be put on a digital map, creating a multi-variate visualization of both geographic and thematic data.*

**What this gives Reach:**

- A view of which philanthropic *themes* (workforce, access, AI literacy, rural education, adult learners, etc.) are funded most heavily, by whom, and where.
- A view of which themes are under-funded but rising — the leading-edge bets.
- A view of where Reach's own thematic strengths align with funder interest — and where they don't (yet).
- And because every funder and every theme has a *place* on the map, all of this becomes one **multivariate visualization**: who, where, and about-what — overlaid.

**This is the payoff slide.** It's where the deck quietly hands the audience something nobody at a peer institution has: *one map of the philanthropic landscape, with Reach's position legible on it.*
